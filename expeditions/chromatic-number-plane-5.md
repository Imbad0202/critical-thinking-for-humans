# Expedition Pack: The Chromatic Number of the Plane is at Least 5 (Forecaster)

`pack_id: chromatic-number-plane-5`

A forecaster pack on a bound, not a final answer. In 2018 an amateur raised the
chromatic number of the plane's lower bound from 4 to 5 with an explicit
1581-vertex graph — but the value itself is still only known to be 5, 6, or 7.
You predict, before each reveal, what the result does and does NOT settle.

## problem

**Statement.** The Hadwiger–Nelson problem asks: what is the minimum number of colours needed to colour every point of the Euclidean plane so that no two points exactly one unit apart share a colour? This minimum is the *chromatic number of the plane* (CNP), written χ. Equivalently: what is the chromatic number of the infinite "unit-distance graph" whose vertices are all points of ℝ² and whose edges join points at distance 1?

**Answer (as of de Grey 2018, and still today).** χ is **not known exactly**. It is one of 5, 6, or 7. The pack's actual result is a *partial* one: Aubrey D.N.J. de Grey proved **χ ≥ 5** by exhibiting an explicit finite unit-distance graph that is not 4-colourable. The smallest such graph in his paper has **1581 vertices** (first-party, abstract of arXiv:1804.02385). The matching upper bound χ ≤ 7 had been known since the 1950s via Isbell's 7-colouring of a hexagonal tiling; the previous lower bound was χ ≥ 4, witnessed by the 7-vertex Moser spindle. So de Grey closed the *4-vs-5* question but left the *5/6/7* window open.

**Accessibility note.** The *statement* is fully elementary — anyone who can colour a map understands it. The *claim to audit* ("this specific finite graph cannot be 4-coloured") is a finite, mechanically checkable assertion: a graph with a few thousand vertices either admits a proper 4-colouring or it does not, and a SAT solver settles it definitively. That is exactly why this is a forecaster pack and not a "trust the proof" pack — the hard object (the graph) is checkable, but the human's job is to forecast *what the checkable fact does and does not settle* about the open parent problem. The trap is concluding "χ = 5" from "χ ≥ 5."

## history

**How long open / who.** Posed around 1950 (attributed to Edward Nelson; Hugo Hadwiger and John Isbell are also associated with the early bounds). The range "4, 5, 6, or 7" — lower bound 4 from the Moser spindle, upper bound 7 from Isbell's hexagonal 7-colouring — sat *unmoved for roughly 60+ years*. The bound 4 ≤ χ ≤ 7 is the textbook status quo that de Grey broke in April 2018. After de Grey's preprint, Terence Tao helped launch **Polymath16** (a crowd-sourced collaboration) to shrink the graphs and reduce computer reliance; per the Polymath threads the smallest 5-chromatic graphs fell well below de Grey's 1581 (e.g. a 510-vertex graph reported by Jean Parts in the project). Crucially, Polymath16 did **not** settle the exact value: χ ∈ {5,6,7} remains open in 2026.

**Dead end 1 — "improve the colouring to push the upper bound below 7."** A natural attack is to find a clever 6- or 5-colouring of the whole plane. This is a *dead end* for resolving the problem downward: no such colouring is known, and de Grey's result proves 5 colours are *necessary*, so the upper bound cannot drop below 5. Decades of attempts to beat Isbell's 7-colouring produced nothing, which is *why* the field spent its energy on the lower bound instead.

**Dead end 2 — "push the lower bound up by hand-building a bigger Moser-spindle gadget."** For ~60 years people tried to glue spindles into a non-4-colourable graph by ingenuity alone. This is a *dead end* at human scale: the certifying graph turns out to need ~thousands of vertices and a non-obvious global structure, far past what hand-construction or intuition reaches. The breakthrough needed *computer search over a parametrised family*, not a cleverer small gadget.

**Dead end 3 — "assume the answer is 4 and look for a 4-colouring algorithm."** A reasonable prior (and the literal status quo lower bound) was that χ might be 4. This is a *dead end*: de Grey's explicit graph is a counterexample, so no 4-colouring of the plane exists. Anyone who bet "4" lost — a cautionary note for the forecaster.

**Dead end 4 — "Polymath16 will surely close the gap to a single number."** Tempting to forecast that a high-powered collaboration resolves 5/6/7. This is a *dead end* as a prediction: Polymath16 shrank graphs and tightened verification but did **not** determine the exact value; even constructing a *6*-chromatic unit-distance graph remains open, let alone proving χ ≤ 6.

## solution_provenance

**Where the verified result lives.** Aubrey D.N.J. de Grey, "The chromatic number of the plane is at least 5," arXiv:1804.02385 (submitted 8 Apr 2018; v2 11 Apr 2018; v3 30 May 2018). Later published in *Geombinatorics* (vol. 28, 2018). The abstract states verbatim that the construction gives "a family of finite unit-distance graphs in the plane that are not 4-colourable" and that "the smallest such graph that we have so far discovered has 1581 vertices."

**How verified.** The core claim is a *finite, machine-checkable* assertion: that a specific ~1581-vertex graph has no proper 4-colouring. This is exactly a Boolean satisfiability (SAT) / graph-colouring instance, and was checked by computer — the Polymath16 first thread notes de Grey's argument "required checking for the nonexistence of particular types of 4-colourings of subgraphs ... that have almost 400 vertices, which requires a computer search," later reduced to ~278-vertex subgraphs. So the non-4-colourability is independently re-verifiable by anyone who re-runs a SAT solver on the published edge list — this is the strongest possible provenance for a finite combinatorial claim. (Marijn Heule and others produced independent/optimised verifications and smaller graphs; I did not confirm Heule's specific numbers first-party.)

**First-party check.** Read directly from arXiv:1804.02385 abstract page: the title, author "Aubrey D.N.J. de Grey," submission date 8 Apr 2018, the three version dates, the phrase "not 4-colourable," and the vertex count **1581**. Read first-party from Gil Kalai's announcement post: the upper bound 7 is "found by Isbell, based on the hexagonal packing with 7 colors"; the Moser spindle is "a unit-distance graph with chromatic number 4"; and "Until recently it was known that the answer can be 4, 5, 6 or 7." Read first-party from the Polymath16 thread: the computer-search verification of non-4-colourability and the goal to reduce computer reliance. I could **not** confirm first-party: the *Geombinatorics* volume/page exactly, de Grey's profession, the 510-vertex Polymath record, or any Heule figure — all flagged below.

## step_graph

- **S0 — Pin the question to a graph-colouring statement.** `representation_shift` Reframe "colour the continuous plane" as "chromatic number of the infinite unit-distance graph on ℝ²," so that *finite subgraphs* become legal evidence: any finite unit-distance graph that needs k colours forces χ ≥ k (by the De Bruijn–Erdős compactness theorem, χ of the infinite graph equals the sup over finite subgraphs). Check: the reduction "find a finite non-4-colourable unit-distance graph ⟹ χ ≥ 5" is a standard, citable theorem; verify it is invoked, not assumed.
- **S1 — Search the known bounds before building.** `search_first` Establish the status quo: upper bound χ ≤ 7 (Isbell hexagonal 7-colouring, ~1950s) and lower bound χ ≥ 4 (Moser spindle, 7 vertices). Check: both are first-party in Kalai's post ("found by Isbell ... 7 colors"; "Moser spindle ... chromatic number 4"); confirm the target is to move the *lower* bound from 4 to 5, not to touch the 7.
- **S2 — Probe whether a small gadget can do it.** `small_case_probe` Ask at n = 7 (Moser spindle) and modest sizes whether hand-built spindle-gluings reach non-4-colourability. They do not; the certifying object is large. Check: the historical 60-year failure of small constructions is itself the evidence that the required graph is big — independently checkable against the literature's stalled lower bound.
- **S3 — Set the abandonment condition for the search.** `kill_criteria` The approach is "computer-search a parametrised family of unit-distance graphs for a 4-chromatic-impossible specimen." Kill condition (implicit but real): if no member of the searched family is non-4-colourable, the approach yields nothing — there is no partial credit. Check: a *positive* hit is self-certifying (the graph either is or isn't 4-colourable), so the kill criterion is symmetric with success — verify the claimed hit, don't trust the search narrative.
- **S4 — Recast the summit as one finite verifiable claim.** `milestone_rewrite` Reduce "χ ≥ 5" to the single mechanical assertion: "graph G (explicit edge list, 1581 vertices) admits no proper 4-colouring." Check: this is a SAT instance — encode vertices×4 colour-variables + edge clauses + at-least-one-colour clauses, run a solver, confirm UNSAT. Independently re-runnable by anyone.
- **S5 — Audit the gap, not just the proof.** `lemma_decomposition` Split the headline into (a) "G is a unit-distance graph" (geometric: every edge is a genuine distance-1 pair — checkable by coordinates), (b) "G is not 4-colourable" (combinatorial: SAT-UNSAT), and (c) "therefore χ ≥ 5" (compactness, S0). Note that (a)+(b)+(c) prove a *lower bound only* — they say nothing about whether 5 colours *suffice*. Check: confirm each sub-claim is verified by a *different* method (geometry vs SAT vs theorem), and explicitly confirm none of them touches the upper bound.
- **S6 — Forecast what remains open.** `shape_question` This bound-narrowing has the shape of other famous problems where a checkable construction moves one bound but the exact value stays open (e.g. lower bounds for Ramsey numbers, or the four-colour theorem's computer-checked-but-narrow flavour). Check: confirm against the literature that post-2018 χ ∈ {5,6,7} is still open and that even a 6-chromatic unit-distance graph is not known — i.e. the result did *not* collapse the window.

## breakthrough

**Step S4 (recasting χ ≥ 5 as a single finite SAT-checkable non-4-colourability claim) is the breakthrough**, enabled by the parametrised-family computer search behind it. It eluded the community for ~60 years because the certifying graph needed ~1581 vertices with non-obvious global structure — far beyond what hand-construction or the small-gadget intuition of S2 could reach — so the field stalled at the spindle's χ ≥ 4 until someone reframed the lower bound as a machine-searchable, machine-verifiable finite object rather than a feat of human ingenuity.
