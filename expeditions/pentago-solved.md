# Expedition Pack: Pentago is a First-Player Win (Climber)

`pack_id: pentago-solved`

A games climb: pentago, a 6x6 board with a rotation twist, was STRONGLY solved
— the game value of every reachable position computed — and the first player
wins with perfect play. You work through what 'strongly solved' means, why
working backward from terminal positions gives exact values, and why the
rotation mechanic explodes the state space to 3x10^15 positions.

## problem

**Statement.** Pentago is a two-player abstract game on a 6×6 board split into four 3×3 quadrants. Each turn a player places a stone, then rotates one quadrant 90° (left or right); the goal is five stones in a row (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal). The question: with perfect play by both sides, what is the game-theoretic value of the initial position — first-player win, draw, or second-player win?

**Answer.** Pentago is a first-player win, but with a precise caveat the title flattens. From the paper's Fig. 1 caption: with perfect play the first player wins with any opening move *except the corners, which tie*. So the value of the start position under best play (first player avoiding corner openings) is a win for player 1. The full game was *strongly* solved: the exact win/loss/tie value of every reachable position was computed, via parallel in-core retrograde analysis in 4 hours on 98,304 threads of NERSC's Cray Edison.

**Accessibility note.** The rules and the claim are trivially statable — anyone can learn Pentago in two minutes and check a single line of five. What is inaccessible to a human is the *evidence*: the value rests on exhaustively evaluating 3,009,081,623,421,558 (~3.0×10¹⁵) symmetry-reduced states. No human can hold that, and no human-playable heuristic substitutes for it. The pack trains a reader to audit *why this method yields exact values* and *why the answer has the corner-tie texture*, not to reproduce the computation.

## history

Pentago is a modern commercial game (Mindtwister, 2005), not a centuries-old open problem, so "how long open" is short: it was simply unsolved until Geoffrey Irving's 2014 result (arXiv:1404.0743). The paper notes prior Pentago programs could search "only to fairly low depths" — the game resisted solution not for lack of attention but because its structure defeats the standard toolkit.

The deeper history is methodological: which games had been solved and how. Connect-Four (Allen, Allis, 1988) was the first nontrivial weak solution, later strongly solved by Tromp; checkers (Schaeffer et al.) was weakly solved after 18 years of out-of-core retrograde analysis. Pentago's obstruction is being *divergent*.

- **Dead end — forward search / knowledge-based strategy:** every prior divergent game solved relied on game-specific knowledge to prune search. The paper states no strong knowledge-based strategies are known for Pentago; pure forward search reaches only low depth against branching factor 97.3. This is a dead end because there is no human-legible structure to lean on.
- **Dead end — classical retrograde analysis as used for checkers:** retrograde analysis works cleanly on *convergent* games where pieces are removed (endgame databases shrink toward the leaves). Pentago is divergent: stones are only added, so position counts *grow* with time and peak near the end. Naively importing the checkers recipe (endgame DB + forward search) is a dead end because there is no small endgame core to anchor on.
- **Dead end — out-of-core (disk-backed) storage:** prior large retrograde analyses streamed to disk. The paper reports doing it entirely in-core, noting an out-of-core implementation "would have been much slower." Treating disk as the natural scratch space is a dead end at this scale.
- **Dead end — ignore the rotation symmetry:** the rotation mechanic gives 288 first moves and a 97.3 average branching factor. Without abstracting over the 256 quadrant-rotations, the branching factor alone makes the traversal a dead end on commodity reasoning; abstraction cuts it to 12.2.

## step_graph

- **S0 — Read the source before trusting the title** `search_first` Fetch arXiv abstract + full PDF; do not infer numbers from secondary summaries. The title says "First Player Win" but the body carries the real claim. Check: every figure in the pack appears verbatim in the PDF text (state count, thread count, branching factors).
- **S1 — Separate "strongly solved" from "weakly solved"** `shape_question` Reframe "who wins?" into "what kind of object is the answer?" Weak = value known from the *start* position with a winning line; strong = value known for *every* position. Pentago is strongly solved: the deliverable is a total function from positions to {win, loss, tie}. Check: the intro defines weakly solved (start) vs strongly solved (any position); the result is the latter, exposed via the database+website.
- **S2 — Name why the standard recipe fails: convergent vs divergent** `kill_criteria` Establish the disqualifier for the checkers-style approach. Convergent games shed pieces so endgame databases shrink toward the leaves; Pentago only *adds* stones, so position counts grow with stone count (peak near 18 stones, Fig. 2). Kill criterion: any method assuming a small endgame core is invalid here. Check: paper's contrast — checkers' ≤10-piece DB at 3.9×10^13 vs Pentago divergence.
- **S3 — See why retrograde analysis still computes *exact* values** `representation_shift` Shift from "search forward and guess" to "induct backward from terminal positions." A 35/36-stone position's value is decided by the rules directly; an n-stone position's value is the minimax over children whose values are already known from the (n+1)-stone slice. Process slices in decreasing stone count and every value is exact by construction, no heuristic. Check: paper's recurrence v_c(s) = max over placements of (immediate win, else max over rotations of h_c) and the slice traversal from 35 stones downward.
- **S4 — Tame the rotation blow-up by abstracting over rotations** `representation_shift` The rotation mechanic gives 288 first moves / 97.3 average branching. Reframe the unit of computation from a single board to a *rotation-abstracted position*: all 256 quadrant-rotations of a board at once (group L = Z4^4), so a "move" no longer pays the rotation branching. This drops effective branching to 12.2 and exposes SIMD parallelism. Check: paper's L = Z4×Z4×Z4×Z4, 256 functions, branching 97.3 → 12.2.
- **S5 — Use symmetry to compute one representative per class** `lemma_decomposition` Decompose the state space by the 8-element dihedral group D4 (4 reflections, 4 rotations); compute one board per equivalence class, a factor-8 saving. Combined with local rotations the full approximate symmetry group G = Z4^4 ⋊ D4 has 2048 elements, organizing which sections must be computed and which are derivable by transform. Check: paper's D4 description and G with 2048 elements; "we compute only those sections which are unique with symmetries removed."
- **S6 — Confront the true scale and the in-core engineering** `milestone_rewrite` Restate the goal as an infrastructure milestone, not a math one: 3,009,081,623,421,558 reduced states must be traversed in reverse stone-order within available RAM. Solved in-core (disk only for a small fraction) on 98,304 threads in 4 hours; asynchronous message passing was needed to fit RAM. Check: exact count and "performed entirely in-core, writing only a small portion ... to disk," 4 hours / 98304 threads.
- **S7 — Read the answer at the right resolution** `small_case_probe` Probe the simplest cases — the opening moves — against the solved database. Result is not a flat "P1 wins": corner openings *tie*, all other openings win for player 1. This is the auditable texture only a strong solution can report. Check: Fig. 1 caption "the first player wins with any opening move except the corners, which tie."

## breakthrough

**S3 is the conceptual breakthrough, enabled by S4.** Retrograde (backward) induction is what makes the values *exact* rather than heuristic: terminal positions are scored by the rules, and every earlier slice is minimax over already-solved successors, so "perfect play" is a computed fact, not an estimate. It eluded easy application because retrograde analysis was the tool for *convergent* games (checkers-style shrinking endgames), and Pentago is divergent — position counts grow toward the end, so there is no small core to start from and the naive instinct is that retrograde analysis "doesn't apply." Irving's move was to run the induction over *stone-count slices* of a divergent game anyway, after the rotation-abstraction of S4 made the per-step branching small enough to be tractable. The non-obvious leap is recognizing that divergence forbids the checkers *workflow* (endgame DB + forward search) but not the underlying backward-induction *principle*.

## audit_targets

- **T1 — "First player wins" is unconditional.** *Objection:* the title asserts a flat first-player win, so the reader may state the value as "P1 wins, full stop." *Resolution:* the Fig. 1 caption restricts this — P1 wins with any opening *except corners, which tie*. An auditor must downgrade the headline to its conditional form; the strong solution is precisely what licenses the exception.
- **T2 — Retrograde analysis only works on convergent games.** *Objection:* a reader who learned retrograde analysis from checkers may insist it cannot apply to a divergent game and call the method invalid. *Resolution:* the recurrence and slice-traversal (S3) are valid on any finite game; divergence kills the endgame-database *workflow*, not backward induction. Audit by checking that values propagate from the 35/36-stone slice downward — direction, not piece-count monotonicity, is what matters.
- **T3 — The ~3×10¹⁵ figure is the raw state count.** *Objection:* one might quote 3.0×10¹⁵ as the number of Pentago positions tout court. *Resolution:* the paper's 3,009,081,623,421,558 is the count *with symmetries removed*, and all but 0.3% are reachable with valid play. The unreduced count is larger; conflating reduced/unreduced or reachable/total is the error to catch.
- **T4 — "Solved" means a human can now play perfectly.** *Objection:* readers equate "solved game" with a memorizable strategy. *Resolution:* strong solution = a database/oracle of exact values for every position; perfect play requires *querying* it (hence perfect-pentago.net), not a human-sized rule. The deliverable is data, not a slogan.
- **T5 — The branching/symmetry numbers are interchangeable.** *Objection:* a reader may blur 288 (first moves), 97.3 (average branching), 12.2 (rotation-abstracted branching), 8 (D4), and 2048 (full group G). *Resolution:* each has a distinct role — verify which quantity each claim depends on; e.g. the in-core feasibility argument leans on the 12.2 reduction and the 2048-element organization, not on 288.
